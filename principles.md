# PRINCIPLES

## P01: The Reception Principle
Communication is measured by the experience produced in the receiver's mind, not by the quality of the transmission. Words are instruments. The effect is the point.

**Mechanism:** The communicator's job is to engineer a specific cognitive state in the target. Every word choice, structure, and example must be evaluated by: "How will this be received?" not "How well did I express it?"

**Test:** If you removed the receiver from the equation and the message still feels "complete," you are writing for yourself, not for them.

## P02: The Worldbuilding Principle
All persuasion operates by constructing, entering, or modifying a shared reality space between communicator and receiver.

**Mechanism:** Humans interpret all new information through existing mental models. Effective communication either fits seamlessly into an existing world or carefully extends one. You cannot inject ideas into a vacuum — you must plant them in soil the receiver already tends.

**Test:** Can you name the specific world your message operates within? If not, it's floating in abstraction.

## P03: The Cognitive Immune System
The mind actively resists unfamiliar ideas, unfamiliar language, and excessive cognitive load.

**Mechanism:** Immune response strength = novelty × complexity × distance from existing beliefs. The immune system deactivates when: existing beliefs are validated first, presentation quality is high, language is simple, and the receiver's defenses are naturally low. Every piece of communication must manage this immune response.

**Test:** If the receiver pushes back harder the more evidence you provide, their immune system is activated. Switch to validation-first.

## P04: The Atomic Agreement Principle
Complex persuasion must be built from simple, non-controversial foundations the audience already accepts.

**Mechanism:** Start with the lowest-information-cost claims. Each agreement ("nod") creates a foundation for the next claim. Stack incrementally. Never start with the controversial claim — start with the obvious one.

**Test:** Could the audience disagree with your opening statement? If yes, you're starting too high.

## P05: The Rooting Principle
Abstract claims become believable only when anchored to concrete, verifiable instances.

**Mechanism:** Examples function as existence proofs. They convert theory into demonstrated reality. More examples = stronger rooting. When no examples exist, a prototype substitutes. When neither exists, the claim is unrootable — wait or build proof.

**Test:** For every claim you make, can you point to a specific instance where it happened? If not, the claim will float.

## P06: The Frame Shift Principle
To change a worldview, first fully enter the existing world, earn agreement, then introduce a modification and root it.

**Mechanism:** Validate → Lower defenses → Introduce deviation → Anchor with examples. Never lead with the deviation. The audience must feel they are discovering the new idea, not being sold it.

**Test:** Does your message start in THEIR world or YOUR world? If yours, invert.

## P07: The Identity Mirror Principle
The most powerful persuasion reflects the target's desired self-image back at them, confirming their identity rather than arguing features.

**Mechanism:** Confirmation bias causes people to favor information that validates who they believe they are. Selling identity ("you are right to want this, and here I am confirming it") bypasses feature-level resistance entirely.

**Test:** Does your communication make the receiver feel SEEN, or does it make them feel SOLD TO?

## P08: The Leverage Point Principle
In any disagreement, identify and address the single foundational assumption the opposing view depends on. Do not fight surface symptoms.

**Mechanism:** Every complex position has one load-bearing assumption. Shifting it collapses the entire structure. Multiple weak arguments dilute force and signal low confidence.

**Test:** Can you state the ONE assumption that, if changed, makes the opposing view impossible? If you can't find it, you don't understand the opposition well enough.

## P09: The Cognitive Hospitality Principle
The effectiveness of an idea is inversely proportional to the cognitive cost of receiving it.

**Mechanism:** Simple language, familiar words, clean structure, concrete stories — all reduce processing overhead. The saved energy gets redirected to absorbing the actual idea. Writing simply is not dumbing down — it is a sign of respect and it scales.

**Test:** Could a smart 15-year-old understand your message? If not, simplify.

## P10: The Consistency Principle
Believability of any constructed reality is proportional to the internal consistency of its details across all scales.

**Mechanism:** The mind checks new information against established world-rules. Inconsistencies break immersion and reactivate the cognitive immune system. Micro-details that maintain consistency create depth and trust.

**Test:** Does every element of your world obey its own rules? Check at the micro level.

## P11: The Availability Heuristic Principle
Humans judge importance by how easily an example comes to mind, not by actual frequency.

**Mechanism:** Concrete, vivid, emotionally charged examples dominate perception. Abstract statistics are processed poorly and forgotten quickly. A single compelling story outweighs any volume of data.

**Test:** After reading your message, what IMAGE comes to the receiver's mind? If the answer is "none," your message has no availability.

## P12: The Dissonance Engine
Presenting a target with two beliefs they hold that are mutually contradictory forces them to resolve the tension — creating an opening for your idea.

**Mechanism:** Create cognitive dissonance by surfacing a conflict between held beliefs. The tension demands resolution. Provide your idea as the resolution. The audience adopts it as relief, not as imposition.

**Test:** Can you name two things your audience believes that conflict with each other? If yes, you have a dissonance opportunity.

## P13: The Dual-Domain Equivalence
Worldbuilding for human readers and worldbuilding for AI models operate through identical mechanisms — constraining a possibility space through context, rules, and examples.

**Mechanism:** Prompts constrain embedding space the way narrative context constrains human interpretation. Few-shot examples = rooting. System instructions = world laws. Specificity = depth. A vague prompt spawns a generic universe. A detailed prompt spawns a rich, specific one.

**Test:** Is your prompt creating a universe or leaving a void? Every unspecified dimension will be filled with generic defaults.
